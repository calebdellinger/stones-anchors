The Good Samaritan — Luke 10:30–37

The Story
Sacrifice means your time, your resources, and your comfort are interruptible.
A priest and a Levite see the beaten man and pass by. They protect their schedules, their cleanliness, their safety. The Samaritan stops. Look at the verbs — this is what sacrifice looks like in motion.

Luke 10:33 (ESV)
"But a Samaritan, as he journeyed, came to where he was, and when he saw him, he had compassion."
Greek splanchnizomai — to be moved in one's bowels, to feel deep gut-level compassion. This is the same word used of Jesus when He sees the crowds. Sacrifice begins not with a decision but with a willingness to feel what others feel.
Luke 10:34 (ESV)
"He went to him and bound up his wounds, pouring on oil and wine. Then he set him on his own animal and brought him to an inn and took care of him."
Oil and wine were expensive medical provisions. Giving up his animal meant he walked. He paid for the man's ongoing care out of his own pocket. Sacrifice is always specific, costly, and inconvenient.
What the Samaritan Gave Up
His schedule — he stopped mid-journey
His supplies — oil, wine, bandages
His comfort — walked so the man could ride
His money — paid the inn and promised more
