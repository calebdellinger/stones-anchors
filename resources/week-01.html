Week 1 · Leadership

Servant leadership

The Lion & the Lamb — authority without humility is just control. Humility without authority is just passivity.

“The only proof of true faith is obedience.” — Andrew Murray. A servant in the ancient world lived inside the master’s oikos — household, not a shift. Below: full ESV verse cards for study, plus Week 1 slides as a PDF.

Scripture · Servanthood

Power, love, and self-control; faith that works; sowing and reaping; the bondservant who stays for love.

2 Timothy 1:7

for God gave us a spirit not of fear but of power and love and self-control.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Spirit — Greek pneuma; here the disposition God gives, not a mere mood.
  • Power — dunamis; ability to act effectively under pressure.
  • Self-control — sōphronismos (verbal noun); sound judgment, disciplined restraint.

Cultural & historical significance

Paul writes Timothy in a context of shame and opposition; “fear” was the default reflex. The triad power–love–discipline maps onto public courage, relational faithfulness, and steady-minded obedience — a profile for gospel servants in a watching world.

James 2:17

So also faith by itself, if it does not have works, is dead.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Faith — pistis; trust that must show itself in concrete loyalty.
  • Works — erga; deeds that embody what one claims to believe.
  • Dead — nekra; lifeless, ineffective — faith that does not issue in action.

Cultural & historical significance

James confronts a pious verbalism that blesses the poor with words but withholds help. In a honor–shame culture, “faith” could be reduced to public claims; James insists the claim must match embodied care.

2 Corinthians 9:6–7

The point is this: whoever sows sparingly will also reap sparingly, and whoever sows bountifully will also reap bountifully.

Each one must give as he has decided in his heart, not reluctantly or under compulsion, for God loves a cheerful giver.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Sows… reap — agricultural metaphor: present generosity shapes future harvest.
  • Cheerful — Greek hilaron; gladly disposed, not grudging.

Cultural & historical significance

Paul gathers funds for Judean believers; the ethic is voluntary, heart-level proportionality rather than public coercion — a model for how servants of Christ invest finite resources in eternal priorities.

Galatians 6:7–9

Do not be deceived: God is not mocked, for whatever one sows, that will he also reap.

For the one who sows to his own flesh will from the flesh reap corruption, but the one who sows to the Spirit will from the Spirit reap eternal life.

And let us not grow weary of doing good, for in due season we will reap, if we do not give up.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Mocked — myktizō; treated as if God will not hold the line on outcomes.
  • Flesh… Spirit — two fields of sowing: self-centered impulse versus Spirit-led obedience.

Cultural & historical significance

Harvest imagery was ubiquitous in agrarian Galatia; Paul turns it into moral eschatology — patience in well-doing because God’s season of reaping is sure.

Exodus 21:5–6

But if the slave plainly says, ‘I love my master, my wife, and my children; I will not go out free,’

then his master shall bring him to God, and he shall bring him to the door or the doorpost. And his master shall bore his ear through with an awl, and he shall be his slave forever.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Bring him to God — likely to the sanctuary or judicial presence; solemnizing the vow.
  • Bore his ear — permanent mark of chosen belonging to the master’s house.

Cultural & historical significance

Covenant law regulated servitude; this provision honored a servant’s free declaration of loyalty. Later readers saw a picture of love-chosen service — not trapped labor but “I want this house.”

Scripture · Leadership

Jesus reverses greatness (Mark 9:35); wisdom literature and Torah fill out vision, decisiveness, accountability, and humility.

Mark 9:35

And he sat down and called the twelve. And he said to them, “If anyone would be first, he must be last of all and servant of all.”

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Servant of all — diakonos; table-servant, runner of errands — lowest posture, highest honor in Christ’s economy.

Cultural & historical significance

Disciples argued over rank; Jesus seats himself as rabbi and flips the ladder. “First” in God’s kingdom runs through service, not swagger.

Proverbs 29:18

Where there is no prophetic vision the people cast off restraint, but blessed is he who keeps the law.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Prophetic vision — Hebrew ḥāzôn; revelation that gives direction, not vague dreaming.
  • Cast off restraint — the people become unbridled without God’s word guiding them.

Cultural & historical significance

Royal and priestly life in Israel depended on torah and prophetic correction; leaders who cannot articulate where God is taking the community invite drift and disorder.

James 1:7–8

For that person must not suppose that he will receive anything from the Lord;

he is a double-minded man, unstable in all his ways.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Double-minded — dipsychos; literally “two-souled,” pulled between God and competing loyalties.

Cultural & historical significance

James ties divided loyalty to failed prayer; leaders who won’t choose cannot steady anyone else.

1 Kings 18:21

And Elijah came near to all the people and said, “How long will you go limping between two different opinions? If the LORD is God, follow him; but if Baal, then follow him.” And the people did not answer him a word.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Limping — uneven gait; Israel’s syncretism made their walk morally hobbled.

Cultural & historical significance

On Carmel, Yahweh versus Baal was not a polite dialogue but a fork in the road for the nation; Elijah forces the crisis into the open.

Matthew 5:37

Let what you say be simply ‘Yes’ or ‘No’; anything more than this comes from evil.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Simply Yes or No — truth-telling without oaths piled on oaths to manipulate hearers.

Cultural & historical significance

Jesus critiques oath-culture where clever language evaded plain integrity; leaders owe followers unvarnished reliability.

Proverbs 27:23

Know well the condition of your flocks, and give attention to your herds,

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Know well — Hebrew yadaʿ; intimate, inspected knowledge, not assumptions.

Cultural & historical significance

Agrarian leadership meant counting cost, spotting weakness early, and tending what you steward — direct parallel to knowing your people’s real condition.

Proverbs 15:1

A soft answer turns away wrath, but a harsh word stirs up anger.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Soft — Hebrew rakh; gentle, yielding in tone without abandoning truth.

Cultural & historical significance

Wisdom literature prizes speech that de-escalates; leaders who cannot govern their words cannot govern a room.

Proverbs 24:16

for the righteous falls seven times and rises again, but the wicked stumble in times of calamity.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Seven times — completeness; repeated failure still met with rising for the righteous.

Cultural & historical significance

Contrast: the wicked lack recovery mechanics under pressure; the righteous’s habit is repentance and return.

Proverbs 10:9

Whoever walks in integrity walks securely, but he who makes his ways crooked will be found out.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Integrity — Hebrew tōm; completeness, moral wholeness in conduct.

Cultural & historical significance

Public life in the ancient city eventually surfaced hidden deals; the proverb bets on exposure for the crooked and steadiness for the upright.

Exodus 18:21

Moreover, look for able men from all the people, men who fear God, who are trustworthy and hate a bribe, and place such men over the people as chiefs of thousands, of hundreds, of fifties, and of tens.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Able men — ḥayil; strength of character and capacity, not mere muscle.
  • Hate a bribe — disqualifies those who can be bought.

Cultural & historical significance

Jethro’s administrative counsel scaled justice for a freed slave nation; delegation under godly criteria prevented Moses’ burnout.

Philippians 2:3

Do nothing from selfish ambition or conceit, but in humility count others more significant than yourselves.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Humility — tapeinophrosynē; lowliness of mind, the opposite of factional pride.

Cultural & historical significance

In a Greco-Roman honor race, Paul commands the church to rank others ahead of self — the same mindset that culminates in Christ’s humiliation and exaltation (2:5–11).

Micah 6:8

He has told you, O man, what is good; and what does the LORD require of you but to do justice, and to love kindness, and to walk humbly with your God?

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Justice — Hebrew mišpāṭ; right order in public life.
  • Kindness — ḥesed; covenant loyalty and steadfast love.
  • Walk humbly — careful, dependent posture before Yahweh.

Cultural & historical significance

Micah compresses torah into three verbs against empty ritualism; it became a summary of the moral life Israel’s God requires.
