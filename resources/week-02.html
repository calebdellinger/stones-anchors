Week 2 · Leadership

Obedience

“The only proof of true faith is obedience.” — Andrew Murray

Anchored in Genesis 22 — the story of Abraham and Isaac — this week examines what true obedience looks like: its cost, its foundation in faith, and its ultimate fulfillment in Christ. Supporting passages from 1 Samuel 15, Hebrews 11, Philippians 2, and Romans 4–5.

Supporting Verse Cards

1 Samuel 15:22

"And Samuel said, 'Has the LORD as great delight in burnt offerings and sacrifices, as in obeying the voice of the LORD? Behold, to obey is better than sacrifice, and to listen than the fat of rams.'"

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Obeying / listen (Hebrew shama) -> to hear attentively with responsive action, not merely to receive sound.
  • Sacrifice (Hebrew zebach) -> slaughtered offering; covenant worship act that can be emptied of meaning without submission.
  • Delight (Hebrew chaphets) -> to take pleasure in; the verse contrasts what God values most.

Cultural & historical significance

In Israel's worship life, animal sacrifice was central and visible, but Samuel confronts the idea that ritual can substitute for covenant loyalty. The prophet speaks into a royal setting where Saul tries to reframe disobedience as worship.

The phrase "fat of rams" points to prized portions of offerings in ancient sacrifice practice. Samuel's rebuke restores the priority: hearing-and-doing God's voice over religious performance.

1 Samuel 15:23

"For rebellion is as the sin of divination, and presumption is as iniquity and idolatry. Because you have rejected the word of the LORD, he has also rejected you from being king."

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Rebellion (Hebrew meri) -> defiance or stubborn resistance against authority.
  • Divination (Hebrew qesem) -> forbidden occult guidance condemned in Israel's covenant law.
  • Rejected (Hebrew ma'as) -> to despise or cast aside; Saul's inner posture mirrors his outward act.

Cultural & historical significance

Samuel equates partial obedience with practices Israel associated with pagan nations. In the ancient Near East, kings often sought omens to secure power; Israel's king was to submit to God's revealed word instead.

This verse frames disobedience as a rival allegiance problem, not a minor procedural failure. The consequence is covenantal kingship loss, reinforcing that authority in Israel remained derivative under God.

Philippians 2:8

"And being found in human form, he humbled himself by becoming obedient to the point of death, even death on a cross."

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Humbled (Greek tapeinoo) -> to lower oneself willingly; Christ's self-emptying posture in action.
  • Obedient (Greek hypekoos) -> one who listens under authority and responds faithfully.
  • Cross (Greek stauros) -> instrument of Roman execution and public shame.

Cultural & historical significance

In the Roman world, crucifixion represented social humiliation and imperial terror, usually reserved for slaves, rebels, and non-citizens. Paul underscores not only that Christ died, but the kind of death He embraced.

By linking obedience to the cross, the passage reframes greatness in a status-driven culture. Jesus models covenant faithfulness through downward movement rather than honor-seeking ascent.

Romans 5:19

"For as by the one man's disobedience the many were made sinners, so by the one man's obedience the many will be made righteous."

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Disobedience (Greek parakoe) -> refusal to hear rightly; hearing beside or against the command.
  • Obedience (Greek hypakoe) -> hearing under authority; responsive trust expressed in action.
  • Made righteous (Greek dikaioi katastathesontai) -> constituted or established as righteous in covenant standing.

Cultural & historical significance

Paul uses representative-head language familiar to covenant and kinship thinking in both Jewish and Greco-Roman settings: one figure's action affects the many bound to him. Adam and Christ are presented as two humanity-defining heads.

The verse provides the theological backbone for Week 2's obedience theme: obedience is not merely moral effort, but redemptive action culminating in Christ's faithful covenant fulfillment.
