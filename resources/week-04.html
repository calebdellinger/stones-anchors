Week 4 · Passion

The Fire Within

“Is not my word like fire, declares the Lord, and like a hammer that breaks the rock in pieces?” — Jeremiah 23:29

What does the Bible say about passion? From the flaming sword at Eden to the blazing eyes of the returning King, Scripture traces a single thread: God is a consuming fire, and those He calls are set ablaze. Below: the canonical arc from Genesis to Revelation, full ESV verse cards, a Hebrew & Greek word study, and the Week 4 slide deck.

The Language of Fire — Hebrew & Greek Roots

Passion is not a modern concept. It is woven into the oldest words of Scripture. Understanding the roots reveals what God means when He burns.

Qin’ah (Hebrew) — Jealousy / Zeal

You shall not bow down to them or serve them, for I the LORD your God am a jealous God.

Exodus 20:5 · English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Qin’ah — jealousy, zeal, burning exclusive devotion. Used of God’s jealousy over Israel — a fire that will not share what it loves.
  • The same root describes Phinehas’s zeal (Numbers 25:11) and the “zeal of the LORD of hosts” that accomplishes His purposes (Isaiah 9:7).
  • This is not petty jealousy. It is the passion of a covenant God who will not be replaced.

Pathos (Greek) — Suffering / The Passion

He presented himself alive to them after his suffering by many proofs, appearing to them during forty days and speaking about the kingdom of God.

Acts 1:3 · English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Pathos — from pascho, to suffer. The theological “Passion of Christ” refers to His suffering and death.
  • Passion, at its deepest root, means the willingness to suffer for what you love. It is not emotion — it is cost.
  • The Passion of Christ is the supreme demonstration: love so intense it absorbs the full weight of its object’s debt.

Zeō (Greek) — To Boil / Fervent

Do not be slothful in zeal, be fervent in spirit, serve the Lord.

Romans 12:11 · English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Zeō — literally, to boil. Not warm enthusiasm, but a rolling, active, overflowing intensity.
  • Paul is not asking for mild engagement. He is commanding a boiling spiritual intensity directed at the Lord.
  • The same root is used of Apollos (Acts 18:25) — a man whose passion for Scripture was visible to everyone around him.

Esh (Hebrew) — Fire

For the LORD your God is a consuming fire, a jealous God.

Deuteronomy 4:24 · English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Esh — the most direct Hebrew word for fire, used over 370 times in the Old Testament.
  • Describes God’s presence (burning bush), His judgment (Sodom), His purification (Isaiah’s coal), and His passion (the pillar of fire).
  • The writer of Hebrews quotes this verse in the New Testament (Hebrews 12:29) — the consuming fire is not an OT concept. It is eternal.

Genesis · The Beginning — The Flaming Sword & the Covenant Fire

From the very first pages of Scripture, fire is God’s signature. The flaming sword at Eden’s gate was not mere punishment — it was passionate protection. Then, in the darkness of Abraham’s night, God Himself passed between the covenant pieces as a smoking firepot and a blazing torch. In ancient covenant-making, both parties walked through — but here, God walked alone. His passion for His promise was so absolute, He took the full weight of the oath upon Himself.

Genesis 3:24

He drove out the man, and at the east of the garden of Eden he placed the cherubim and a flaming sword that turned every way to guard the way to the tree of life.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Cultural & historical significance

The flaming sword (lahat hahereb) is not a weapon of destruction but a guardian of the sacred. God’s passion for holiness blazed so intensely that He stationed divine fire to protect what was most precious. This is the first appearance of fire in Scripture — and it is already a statement about the nature of God.

Genesis 15:17–18

When the sun had gone down and it was dark, behold, a smoking fire pot and a flaming torch passed between these pieces. On that day the LORD made a covenant with Abram.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Cultural & historical significance

In ancient Near Eastern covenant rituals, both parties walked between the divided animals — a self-maledictory oath: “May what happened to these animals happen to me if I break this covenant.” God alone passed through. He bound Himself to the promise unilaterally. The fire is not decoration; it is the signature of a God whose passion for His people is unconditional and irrevocable.

Exodus · The Wilderness — The Burning Bush & the Pillar of Fire

Moses was 80 years old, a forgotten shepherd in the backside of the desert, when God’s passion interrupted his ordinary day. The bush burned — but was not consumed. This is the paradox of divine fire: intense enough to arrest your entire attention, yet gentle enough not to destroy what it inhabits. Then, for forty years, every night, God became a pillar of fire. Not once did it go out.

Exodus 3:2–4

The angel of the LORD appeared to him in a flame of fire out of the midst of a bush. He looked, and behold, the bush was burning, yet it was not consumed. And God called to him out of the bush, “Moses, Moses!”

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Cultural & historical significance

The Sinai peninsula was full of desert scrub. Fires were not unusual. What arrested Moses was not the fire itself — it was that the bush was not consumed. Divine fire does not destroy what it inhabits; it illuminates it. This is the pattern for every person God calls: He burns in you without burning you up.

Exodus 13:21

And the LORD went before them by day in a pillar of cloud to lead them along the way, and by night in a pillar of fire to give them light, that they might travel by day and by night.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Cultural & historical significance

For forty years — approximately 14,600 nights — the fire burned. This was not a metaphor. It was a visible, physical, daily reality for an entire generation. God’s passion for His people was so persistent that He refused to let them walk in darkness for a single night. That is the nature of divine zeal: it does not tire.

1 Kings 18 · The Prophet — The God Who Answers by Fire

Elijah stood alone against 450 prophets of Baal on Mount Carmel. He had soaked the altar with water — twelve jars, filling the trench — and then prayed a single, 63-word prayer. The fire of the LORD fell and consumed the burnt offering, the wood, the stones, the dust, and licked up the water. The passion of God is not diminished by impossibility. It is revealed by it.

1 Kings 18:37–38

“Answer me, O LORD, answer me, that this people may know that you, O LORD, are God, and that you have turned their hearts back.” Then the fire of the LORD fell and consumed the burnt offering and the wood and the stones and the dust, and licked up the water that was in the trench.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Cultural & historical significance

Baal was the Canaanite storm god — the god of lightning and fire. Elijah’s challenge was a direct confrontation on Baal’s home turf. When the fire of the LORD fell on a water-soaked altar, it was a declaration: there is no god of fire but the LORD. His passion for His name is absolute.

Isaiah 6 · The Throne Room — The Coal from the Altar

Isaiah saw the Lord seated on a throne, high and lifted up, and the seraphim crying “Holy, holy, holy.” His response was not worship — it was collapse. “Woe is me! For I am lost.” Then one of the seraphim flew to him with a burning coal from the altar and touched his mouth. The fire that undoes you is the same fire that commissions you.

Isaiah 6:6–8

Then one of the seraphim flew to me, having in his hand a burning coal that he had taken with tongs from the altar. And he touched my mouth and said: “Behold, this has touched your lips; your guilt is taken away, and your sin atoned for.” And I heard the voice of the Lord saying, “Whom shall I send, and who will go for us?” Then I said, “Here I am! Send me.”

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Cultural & historical significance

The coal came from the altar — the place of sacrifice and atonement. The fire that purified Isaiah was not arbitrary; it was the fire of substitutionary death. Purification by fire is always costly. And the result of that purification was not rest but commission: “Here I am! Send me.” Passion that has been purified always produces availability.

Jeremiah 20 · The Reluctant Prophet — Fire Shut Up in My Bones

Jeremiah tried to quit. He had been mocked, beaten, and thrown in a cistern. He resolved to speak no more in God’s name. But the word of God was like a fire shut up in his bones — he was weary of holding it in, and he could not. This is one of the most honest verses in Scripture: passion is not always comfortable. Sometimes it is a fire you cannot contain even when you want to.

Jeremiah 20:9

If I say, “I will not mention him, or speak any more in his name,” there is in my heart as it were a burning fire shut up in my bones, and I am weary of holding it in, and I cannot.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Cultural & historical significance

Jeremiah is often called the “weeping prophet” — a man who did not want the calling he received. He argued with God, complained about his birth, and tried to silence himself. But the fire was not his to control. This is the most important thing to understand about divine passion: it is not generated by the person who carries it. It is placed there. The question is only whether you will yield to it or fight it.

Daniel 3 · The Furnace — The Fourth Man in the Fire

Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego were thrown into a furnace heated seven times hotter than normal — so hot it killed the soldiers who threw them in. But inside the furnace, the king saw four men walking, unbound, and the fourth looked like a son of the gods. The fire that was meant to destroy them became the place where they met the divine. Passion does not protect you from the furnace. It meets you inside it.

Daniel 3:24–25

Then King Nebuchadnezzar was astonished and rose up in haste. He declared to his counselors, “Did we not cast three men bound into the fire?” They answered and said to the king, “True, O king.” He answered and said, “But I see four men unbound, walking in the midst of the fire, and they are not hurt; and the appearance of the fourth is like a son of the gods.”

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Cultural & historical significance

The Babylonian furnace was a kiln used for metalworking — an industrial instrument of execution. The three men entered bound. They walked out free, with not even the smell of smoke on them. The fire burned the ropes but not the men. This is the pattern of divine fire in the life of the believer: it consumes what binds you and leaves what is essential untouched.

Acts 2 · Pentecost — Tongues as of Fire

Fifty days after the resurrection, 120 people were gathered in an upper room in Jerusalem. Suddenly a sound like a mighty rushing wind filled the house, and divided tongues as of fire appeared and rested on each one of them. This was the fulfillment of John the Baptist’s promise: “He will baptize you with the Holy Spirit and fire.” The fire of God had moved from the altar to the person.

Acts 2:3–4

And divided tongues as of fire appeared to them and rested on each one of them. And they were all filled with the Holy Spirit and began to speak in other tongues as the Spirit gave them utterance.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Cultural & historical significance

The word divided is significant: each person received their own tongue of fire. The Holy Spirit did not fall on the group as a unit — He rested on each individual. This is the democratization of divine fire. In the Old Testament, the Spirit came upon specific people for specific tasks. At Pentecost, He came upon all flesh — fulfilling Joel’s prophecy (Joel 2:28). The fire of God is no longer reserved for prophets and kings. It is for every believer.

Acts 2:17 (Joel 2:28)

“And in the last days it shall be, God declares, that I will pour out my Spirit on all flesh, and your sons and your daughters shall prophesy, and your young men shall see visions, and your old men shall dream dreams.”

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Cultural & historical significance

Peter’s first sermon is a direct quotation of Joel 2:28. The “last days” began at Pentecost — we are living in them now. The pouring out of the Spirit is not a one-time historical event; it is the ongoing reality of the age of the church. The fire is still falling. The question is whether we are positioned to receive it.

Romans & Hebrews · The Epistles — Fan the Flame

The Greek word translated “fervent” in Romans 12:11 is zeō — literally, to boil. Paul is not asking for mild enthusiasm. He is commanding a boiling, overflowing spiritual intensity. The writer of Hebrews anchors this in the nature of God Himself: “our God is a consuming fire.” Paul’s charge to Timothy is equally urgent: “fan into flame the gift of God.” The Greek word is anazōpureō — to stir up a fire that has begun to die down. Passion can be neglected. It must be tended.

Romans 12:11

Do not be slothful in zeal, be fervent in spirit, serve the Lord.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Fervent — Greek zeō, to boil. The command is to maintain a boiling spiritual intensity, not a comfortable warmth.
  • Slothful — Greek okneros, shrinking back, hesitating. The opposite of passion is not hatred — it is hesitation.
  • The structure of the verse is a command, not a suggestion. Paul places “be fervent in spirit” alongside “love genuinely” and “serve the Lord” as equal imperatives.

Hebrews 12:29

For our God is a consuming fire.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Cultural & historical significance

The writer of Hebrews quotes Deuteronomy 4:24 in the context of the new covenant. He is making a deliberate point: the God of the new covenant is not gentler or less intense than the God of Sinai. He is the same consuming fire. The difference is not the fire — it is that we now approach through the blood of Jesus, which gives us access to the fire without being destroyed by it.

2 Timothy 1:6

For this reason I remind you to fan into flame the gift of God, which is in you through the laying on of my hands.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Etymology & key words

  • Fan into flame — Greek anazōpureō: to stir up a dying fire, to rekindle embers. The implication is that the fire was already there but had diminished.
  • Paul is writing to Timothy from prison, facing execution. His final letters are not about survival — they are about fire. He wants Timothy to burn.
  • The gift of God is not self-sustaining. It requires intentional tending — prayer, Scripture, community, obedience. Neglect it and it dims. Feed it and it blazes.

Revelation 1 & 19 · The Consummation — Eyes Like a Flame of Fire

The Bible ends where it begins — with fire. But now the fire has a face. John sees the risen Christ and is so overwhelmed he falls as if dead. The eyes of Jesus are described as a flame of fire — not a metaphor for anger, but a description of penetrating, all-seeing, all-knowing love. Jesus said, “I came to cast fire on the earth, and how I wish it were already kindled!” The passion of Christ is not past tense. It is present. It is coming. And it is fire.

Revelation 1:14–15

The hairs of his head were white, like white wool, like snow. His eyes were like a flame of fire, his feet were like burnished bronze, refined in a furnace, and his voice was like the roar of many waters.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Cultural & historical significance

John is writing to seven churches under Roman persecution. He is not describing a gentle shepherd. He is describing a conquering King whose eyes see everything — whose gaze penetrates every compromise, every fear, every hidden thing. The fire in His eyes is not wrath toward His people; it is the same fire that burned in the bush: consuming what is false, illuminating what is true.

Revelation 19:12

His eyes are like a flame of fire, and on his head are many diadems, and he has a name written that no one knows but himself.

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Cultural & historical significance

This is the return of the King — not the suffering servant of Isaiah 53, but the Warrior-King of Revelation 19. The fire in His eyes is the same fire that has been burning throughout all of Scripture: the fire of the covenant, the fire of the altar, the fire of Pentecost. It has never gone out. It is coming back. The question the whole Bible asks is: when those eyes find you, will they find a fire burning in return?

Luke 12:49

I came to cast fire on the earth, and would that it were already kindled!

English Standard Version

Blue Letter Bible · Bible Hub

Cultural & historical significance

This is one of the most raw, unguarded statements Jesus ever made. “Would that it were already kindled!” — a cry of longing, of urgency, of divine impatience. Jesus was not a dispassionate teacher. He was a man on fire, moving toward a cross with single-minded intensity, longing for the day when the fire He came to cast would spread across the whole earth. This is the passion of the Son of God. It is not contained. It is not calm. It burns.
