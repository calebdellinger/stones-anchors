## Slide 2: The Disciple & The Leader — The Good Samaritan
*(Advance to Slide 2)*

**Teaching:**
If you want to see the practical cost of sacrificial love, look at the Good Samaritan in Luke 10. A man is beaten, robbed, and left for dead. A priest and a Levite—the religious leaders of the day—see him, but they pass by on the other side. They preserve their own schedules, their own cleanliness, their own safety. 

But the Samaritan stops. Look at the verbs in Luke 10:33-34: He *saw*, he *had compassion*, he *went*, he *bandaged*, he *poured*, he *put him on his own animal*, he *brought him*, he *took care of him*. 

Sacrifice as a disciple and a leader means your time, your resources, and your comfort are interruptible. It means stepping into someone else's mess and bearing the cost of their restoration.

*   **Luke 10:33** — *"But a Samaritan, as he journeyed, came to where he was, and when he saw him, he had compassion."*
    *   *Preceding verse (10:32):* The Levite passes by on the other side. *Following verse (10:34):* He goes to him and bandages his wounds. *Context:* Samaritans and Jews despised each other. The Samaritan crosses ethnic, religious, and social lines, risking his own safety on a dangerous road to help an enemy.

*   **Luke 10:34** — *"He went to him and bound up his wounds, pouring on oil and wine. Then he set him on his own animal and brought him to an inn and took care of him."*
    *   *Preceding verse (10:33):* The Samaritan has compassion. *Following verse (10:35):* He pays the innkeeper two denarii. *Context:* Oil and wine were expensive medical provisions. Giving up his animal meant he had to walk. He pays for the man's ongoing care out of his own pocket.

**Discussion Prompt:**
As a leader in your workplace or community, whose "mess" are you currently avoiding because it would cost too much of your time or comfort to step into?

## Slide 3: The Husband & The Father — Ephesians 5
*(Advance to Slide 3)*

**Teaching:**
If the Good Samaritan shows us the breadth of sacrifice, Ephesians 5 shows us the depth of it inside the home. Paul gives husbands the highest, most demanding standard of love in the entire Bible: "Love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave Himself up for her."

What does that mean practically? It means you die to your preferences, your ego, and your right to be right. It means your primary job as a husband and father is to lay down your life so that your wife and children can flourish. 

Paul says Christ did this to "sanctify her" and present her radiant. A sacrificial husband takes responsibility for the spiritual climate of his home. He absorbs the hits, he initiates the apologies, and he leads the way in repentance.

*   **Ephesians 5:25** — *"Husbands, love your wives, as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her,"*
    *   *Preceding verse (5:24):* As the church submits to Christ, wives should submit to their husbands. *Following verse (5:26):* That he might sanctify her. *Context:* In the Greco-Roman world, husbands had absolute authority and virtually no commanded duties toward their wives. Paul radically subverts this by commanding husbands to imitate the ultimate self-emptying sacrifice of the cross.

*   **Ephesians 5:26** — *"that he might sanctify her, having cleansed her by the washing of water with the word,"*
    *   *Preceding verse (5:25):* Christ gave Himself up for the church. *Following verse (5:27):* So that He might present the church to Himself in splendor. *Context:* The goal of sacrificial love is not just making someone happy; it is their holiness and flourishing.

**Discussion Prompt:**
What is one specific area in your marriage or parenting where you need to "give yourself up" this week for the flourishing of your family?

## Slide 4: The Ransom — The Root of Sacrifice
*(Advance to Slide 4)*

**Teaching:**
We cannot sustain sacrificial love by sheer willpower. We will eventually burn out and become bitter. The only way to live a life of sacrifice is to constantly look at the One who sacrificed Himself for us. 

In Mark 10:45, Jesus says, "For even the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many." 

And in Romans 12:1, Paul says that *because* of God's mercy, our only logical response is to offer our bodies as a "living sacrifice." We sacrifice for our wives, our kids, our employees, and our neighbors because Christ first paid the ransom for us.

*   **Mark 10:45** — *"For even the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many."*
    *   *Preceding verse (10:44):* Whoever would be first among you must be slave of all. *Following verse (10:46):* They come to Jericho. *Context:* Jesus redefines greatness. True leadership is not about how many people serve you, but how many people you serve. "Ransom" (Greek *lutron*) was the price paid to buy a slave's freedom.

*   **Romans 12:1** — *"I appeal to you therefore, brothers, by the mercies of God, to present your bodies as a living sacrifice, holy and acceptable to God, which is your spiritual worship."*
    *   *Preceding verse (11:36):* For from him and through him and to him are all things. *Following verse (12:2):* Do not be conformed to this world. *Context:* After 11 chapters of explaining the gospel, Paul turns to the practical application. A "living sacrifice" is an oxymoron—sacrifices usually die. But God wants us alive, continually offering our daily actions to Him.

**Closing Challenge:**
Sacrifice is the currency of the kingdom. Where is God asking you to spend it this week?
