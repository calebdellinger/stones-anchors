# 1 - Week 1 · Servant Leadership

Welcome to Week 1 of our Stones & Anchors Men's Bible Study. We're diving into servant leadership, which isn't just about managing people. It's about leading with strength under God, humility before God, and a heart that serves rather than controls. This study will challenge us to rethink what leadership truly looks like in our homes, workplaces, and churches. What areas of your life do you feel God is calling you to lead in a new way?

# 2 - The Lion and the Lamb

We start with a powerful image: the Lion and the Lamb. This picture helps us understand that biblical leadership holds two seemingly opposite qualities together: strength and humility. Jesus embodies both perfectly. He is the Lion of Judah, full of authority and courage, as Revelation 5:5 and Isaiah 31:4 show us. But He is also the Lamb of God, humble and sacrificial, as John 1:29 and Isaiah 53:7 reveal. When we separate these, we get into trouble. Authority without humility becomes control, and humility without authority becomes passive. True leadership, like Christ's, balances both. Where in your life do you find yourself leaning too much on one side, either control or passivity? Revelation 5:5: And one of the elders said to me, “Weep no more; behold, the Lion of the tribe of Judah, the Root of David, has conquered, so that he can open the scroll and its seven seals.” Significance: Jesus is identified as the powerful, conquering King. Isaiah 31:4: For thus the Lord said to me, “As a lion or a young lion growls over his prey, and when a band of shepherds is called out against him he is not terrified by their shouting or daunted by their noise, so the Lord of hosts will come down to fight on Mount Zion and on its hill.” Significance: God's unwavering strength and protection are compared to a lion. Revelation 19:15-16: From his mouth comes a sharp sword with which to strike down the nations, and he will rule them with an iron rod. He will tread the winepress of the fury of the wrath of God the Almighty. On his robe and on his thigh he has a name written, King of kings and Lord of lords. Significance: Christ's ultimate authority and kingship are affirmed. John 1:29: The next day he saw Jesus coming toward him, and said, “Behold, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world!” Significance: Jesus is presented as the sacrificial offering for sin. Isaiah 53:7: He was oppressed, and he was afflicted, yet he opened not his mouth; like a lamb that is led to the slaughter, and like a sheep that before its shearers is silent, so he opened not his mouth. Significance: The suffering servant's silent submission to sacrifice is foretold. 1 Peter 1:18-19: knowing that you were ransomed from the futile ways inherited from your forefathers, not with perishable things such as silver or gold, but with the precious blood of Christ, like that of a lamb without blemish or spot. Significance: Our redemption is through Christ's perfect, sacrificial blood. This balance of strength and humility is key as we move to understanding where a servant truly belongs.

# 3 - Servants Belong to the House

Building on that balance of strength and humility, let's talk about belonging. A servant in Scripture wasn't just an employee; he was part of the master's household. His identity, provision, and safety came from belonging to that house, not from his own efforts. This means Kingdom leadership starts with knowing whose house you belong to. We don't build around our own ownership first. Instead, we live from the covering, order, and provision of our Master. This foundational belonging then empowers us to carry His authority. What does it mean for you to truly belong to God's household, and how does that shape your sense of identity? Ephesians 2:19: So then you are no longer strangers and aliens, but you are fellow citizens with the saints and members of the household of God. Significance: Believers are part of God's family, not outsiders. Galatians 4:7: So you are no longer a slave, but a son, and if a son, then an heir through God. Significance: Our status changes from slave to son, with all the rights of an heir. This sense of belonging is crucial for understanding the authority we then carry.

# 4 - Servants Carry the Master's Authority

Because we belong to the Master's house, we also carry His authority. In the ancient world, a trusted servant could speak and act in the master's name. For us, God's people, this means more than just talking about Him. It means moving in faith-filled obedience. God gives us power, love, and a sound mind, not fear, as 2 Timothy 1:7 reminds us. And James 2:17 makes it clear: faith without works is dead. Our authority isn't passive; it acts. This active obedience leads us to a crucial posture in our walk with God. Where are you holding back from acting on your faith, and what keeps you from moving with God's authority? 2 Timothy 1:7: for God gave us a spirit not of fear but of power and love and self-control. Significance: God empowers us with strength and sound judgment, not timidity. James 2:17: So also faith by itself, if it does not have works, is dead. Significance: True faith is demonstrated through action, not just belief. John 20:21: Jesus said to them again, “Peace be with you. As the Father has sent me, even so I am sending you.” Significance: We are commissioned with the same sending authority as Jesus. Acts 4:29: And now, Lord, look upon their threats and grant to your servants to continue to speak your word with all boldness. Significance: Believers pray for boldness to speak God's word despite opposition. This active, bold authority is rooted in our dependency on God.

# 5 - Dependency Beats Autonomy

This active obedience flows from a place of dependency, not autonomy. The heart of servanthood is surrendering our self-rule. A servant aligns his will with the Master's purpose. It's about a posture of 'Hineni' — 'Here I am,' available, attentive, and ready for God. Biblical leadership isn't about radical independence; it's about radical availability to God. Our strength grows when we let go of our own direction and embrace His. This surrender of self-rule leads us to a deeper kind of devotion. What areas of your life are you still trying to control, rather than surrendering to God's direction? John 15:5: I am the vine; you are the branches. Whoever abides in me and I in him, he it is that bears much fruit, for apart from me you can do nothing. Significance: Our fruitfulness comes solely from our connection to Christ. Romans 12:1: I appeal to you therefore, brothers, by the mercies of God, to present your bodies as a living sacrifice, holy and acceptable to God, which is your spiritual worship. Significance: We are called to offer our entire lives as an act of worship. Galatians 2:20: I have been crucified with Christ and I no longer live, but Christ lives in me. The life I now live in the body, I live by faith in the Son of God, who loved me and gave himself for me. Significance: Our identity and life are now defined by Christ living through us. Philippians 2:13: for it is God who works in you, both to will and to work for his good pleasure. Significance: God empowers both our desires and our actions for His purposes. This radical availability then shapes our devotion to Him.

# 6 - Devotion Is a Chosen Bond

We've talked about how true servanthood means radical availability to God. Now, let's dig into devotion. Devotion isn't just some imposed duty; it's a chosen bond, like the bondservant in Scripture who chose lasting devotion out of love. Following Jesus means we willingly surrender our story, our future, and our resources to Him. What we entrust to Him isn't lost; it returns as fruit, far beyond what any self-made system could produce. This devotion is costly, yes, but the return on that surrender is greater than anything a self-directed life can offer. Servanthood chooses the Master's house because it trusts the Master's character. This kind of devotion, this chosen bond, is foundational for the leadership we're called to. This chosen bond then leads us to a clear vision for our work.2 Corinthians 9:6–7: Whoever sows sparingly reaps sparingly, and whoever sows bountifully reaps bountifully. God loves a cheerful giver. Significance: All our work, regardless of its nature, is ultimately service to Christ and should be done with excellence.

# 7 - Vision Sees the Build Before the Start

So, we've laid the groundwork of devotion. Now, let's talk about vision. A leader sees the finished build before the first shovel even hits the dirt. Vision gives the team a clear destination, a reason for their work, and a way to measure progress. Without it, men become reactive, scattered, and easily discouraged. Strategic thinking isn't just abstract dreaming. It's the discipline of seeing where the work needs to go, anticipating obstacles, and communicating the path clearly. This clarity allows others to move with confidence. Proverbs 29:18 tells us, 'Where there is no vision, the people perish.' A clear vision keeps us from perishing. But vision alone isn't enough; we also need decisiveness.Proverbs 29:18: Where there is no vision, the people perish. Significance: Without a clear direction, people lose purpose and direction.Hebrews 11:1: Faith is the assurance of things hoped for and the conviction of things not seen. Significance: Vision requires faith, believing in what is not yet visible.Philippians 3:13–14: Forgetting what lies behind, press on toward the goal. Significance: A clear vision helps us focus and move forward, leaving past failures behind.

# 8 - Decisiveness Breaks Paralysis

We've seen how vision sets the course. Now, decisiveness breaks paralysis. Leadership cannot live in endless hesitation. A leader gathers what he needs, then he makes the call. Endless hesitation drains momentum from any mission. Timely decisions keep things moving, even when conditions aren't perfect. Good leadership doesn't wait forever for perfect clarity. Analysis is important, but analysis paralysis can kill a project faster than a flawed decision made on time. James 1:8 warns us that a double-minded man is unstable in all his ways. Elijah confronted divided loyalty, telling the people to choose whom they would follow. Decisiveness isn't recklessness. It's clear-minded commitment after honest discernment, without instability. And once a decision is made, we move to accountability.James 1:8: A double-minded man is unstable in all his ways. Significance: Indecision leads to instability and a lack of progress.1 Kings 18:21: Elijah confronts divided loyalty: if the Lord is God, follow Him. Significance: Leaders must call people to clear commitment, not divided loyalties.Proverbs 16:9: The heart of man plans his way, but the Lord establishes his steps. Significance: While we plan, God ultimately directs our path, requiring us to act.1 Corinthians 14:33: God is not a God of confusion but of peace. Significance: Decisiveness brings order and peace, reflecting God's nature.Ephesians 5:15–17: Walk wisely, making the best use of the time, and understand the Lord's will. Significance: Wise leaders make timely decisions, understanding God's purpose for the moment.

# 9 - Accountability Owns the Outcome

We've talked about vision and decisiveness. Now, accountability owns the outcome. Real leadership refuses excuses, rejects scapegoats, and tells the truth about what happened. Accountability means owning the miss, naming the system failure, and bearing the weight of correction. Leaders earn trust when they take responsibility before they claim credit. Their word becomes believable because it's anchored in honesty. A leader who can say, 'I was wrong,' builds more credibility than one who always tries to look right. Matthew 5:37 says, 'Let what you say be simply 'Yes' or 'No.'' Ownership isn't weakness; it's the moral strength to carry reality without distortion. This strength also requires emotional intelligence.Matthew 5:37: Let what you say be simply “Yes” or “No.” Significance: Accountability demands honesty and integrity in our words.Proverbs 27:23: Know the condition of your flocks and pay careful attention to what is under your care. Significance: Leaders must be diligent in understanding and caring for those they lead.James 5:16: Confess your sins to one another and pray for one another. Significance: Open confession fosters humility and strengthens community, crucial for accountability.1 Corinthians 4:2: It is required of stewards that they be found faithful. Significance: Leaders are stewards, and faithfulness in their responsibilities is paramount.

# 10 - Emotional Intelligence Guards Relationships

So, we've covered accountability. Now, emotional intelligence guards relationships. Leaders must know their own triggers, blind spots, and patterns. Men who can't read themselves usually misread everyone else too. Leadership isn't just about direction; it's about the stewardship of people. Emotional intelligence helps a leader read the room, understand what motivates men differently, and choose a tone that brings clarity instead of unnecessary heat. Empathy isn't softness. It's the disciplined ability to respond in a way that protects both the mission and the relationship. Proverbs 15:1 reminds us, 'A soft answer turns away wrath, but harsh words stir up anger.' # 11 - Resilience Stands Back Up

Every mission faces setbacks, brothers. Resilience is about standing back up when you get knocked down. A leader's steadiness in crisis often becomes the team's stability. You see, plans fall apart, pressure increases, deadlines move. But a resilient leader becomes the calm in that storm. He keeps his footing, regains momentum, and helps the men around him recover instead of collapse. Proverbs 24:16 tells us the righteous fall seven times and rise again. This isn't about pretending nothing happened. It's about absorbing the hit without surrendering the mission. This ability to recover is crucial, and it flows from how we manage our emotions, as we discussed on the last slide. And that brings us to the bedrock of all leadership. # 12 - Integrity Makes Leadership Credible

Integrity makes leadership credible. Men will not fully follow a leader whose private life and public words do not agree. Leadership gains influence through trust, and trust is built through character. Integrity means doing what is right even when it's inconvenient, expensive, or invisible to everyone else. It keeps authority from becoming manipulation. Proverbs 10:9 says whoever walks in integrity walks securely. A leader's word becomes weighty when others know he pays the same price in secret that he asks from others in public. This kind of character is essential if we want to multiply our efforts. This is a natural progression from resilience, because it's hard to stand back up if your foundation is shaky. Where in your life do you find it hardest to maintain integrity when no one is watching?

# 13 - Delegation Multiplies the Mission

Delegation multiplies the mission. A boss controls people, but a leader develops them and entrusts real responsibility. This isn't about avoiding work; it's how leadership scales the mission, prevents burnout, and trains the next generation of able men to carry meaningful weight. Look at Exodus 18:21, where Jethro tells Moses to appoint able men over groups. The point isn't just efficiency; it's shared strength. Authority entrusted wisely creates health in both the mission and the men carrying it. When we give others room to own real tasks, the work expands, strength is reinforced, and future leaders are formed. This is a critical aspect of humble leadership, and it flows directly from the integrity we just discussed. # 14 - Humility Knows Who You Are

He can confidently step into his role, knowing that without Jesus, he has no lasting strength. This kind of humility keeps us from pride, but also from insecurity. It's a strong position because it's honest about both our gifts and our weaknesses. This is a critical aspect of humble leadership.
